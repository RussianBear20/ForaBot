Chassis Wiring

This document contains the wiring for the breadboard. Note that it is up to you to determine how you want to mount the components to the breadboard. This document merely serves as a guideline that tells you the connections that need to be made. Note that the pinout for the Raspberry Pi can be found at the following URL: pinout.xyz. A simplified pinout graphic provided by pinout.xyz is shown below.

Step #1: Create the +12V Rail

Before continuing, make sure that you have looked at the wiring diagrams for the power connector jack and SPST switch. These diagrams can be found in the datasheets for those components. Also, it is recommended that you use pre-crimped wires for the wires of the +12V rail.

  1. Solder the connector Jack for the power supply. Note that pin #1 is the center pin and pin #2 is the other pin. Assuming that you have a center-positive power supply:
  1. Solder a stranded wire to pin #1. This will be the positive wire for your +12V rail.
  2. Solder two stranded wires to pin #2, preferably of different colors (e.g. black and brown). One of these wires will be the negative wire for your +12V rail and the other wire will be the wire that is used to ground the chassis.
  1. Strip the end of the wire that will be used for the chassis ground and wrap the exposed wire around a machine screw. Attach this screw to the chassis and tighten it with a screw plate. 
  1. IMPORTANT: This wire is used to prevent users from electrocuting themselves in the event that they touch the chassis when a live wire is touching the chassis. Ensure that the screw is fastened to the chassis properly and check continuity with between the chassis (e.g. using the machine screw you just attached) and pin #2 of the power jack.
  1. Connect the negative wire directly to the -12V rail on the board using the screw terminal, (alternatively if using a breadboard, crimp the negative wire if it is not already crimped for the -12V rail and insert it into a two-pin housing.)
  2. Connect the positive wire from the connector jack to the power switch (i.e. SPST switch) Assuming that you are using the SPST switch in the parts list, you will need to use pin 2 and pin 3. One pin will connect to the connector jack and the other to the screw terminal on the pcb - the choice of connections will depend on which way you want the switch to face once it has been put inside its 3D-printed housing. Solder a second wire to the pin that is still unsoldered. Connect this wire directly to the +12V rail on the board using a screw terminal, (alternatively if using a breadboard, connect this wire to the second port of the two-pin housing referenced in step 3.)
  1. Note that pin 3 is used because it bypasses the switch’s LED. As such, the switch will not light up once everything is wired. If you want to use the LED, then you will need to use a 14V power supply since the LED causes a 2V drop. Along with the changed power supply, pins 2 and 4 should be used as opposed to pins 2 and 3.
  1. Mount the power switch (i.e. SPST switch) and connector jack to the chassis via their 3D-printed housings.
  2. If using a breadboard, connect the two-pin header to the breadboard to create the +12V power rail.

Step #2: Create the +5V Rail

The +5V rail is created via a buck converter that attaches to the +12V rail. The Adafruit UBEC board serves as the buck converter for the foram chassis.

  1. Take an Adafruit UBEC board and cut off the housing that is currently attached to the output. Crimp the wires at the input and output of the buck converter. Connect two-pin housings to the input and output wires of the buck converter. Connect the input of the buck converter to the +12V rail and the output of the buck converter to the breadboard to create the +5V rail.

Step #3: Create the Raspberry Pi power cable

  1. Take an Adafruit UBEC board and cut off the housing that is currently attached to the output. Crimp the input wires of the UBEC board and attach them to a two-pin housing. Connect the input of the UBEC board to the +12V rail and connect the output of the board to the USB-to-Screw Terminal cable.

Step #4: Solder the Adafruit LED Ring & TB6612FNG Motor Boards

  1. The Adafruit LED ring needs to have its “data input” “power +5V” and “gnd” pins soldered.
  2. The TB6612FNG motor boards are soldered using header pins

Note that Raspberry Pi pins in this document are labeled as: BCM (Rpi Pin #xx). As an example, GPIO 21 would be written as “GPIO 21 (Rpi Pin #40)”. Note that there are two pictures of an assembled PCB  in the Documentation folder. In these pictures, the +12V rail is on the left (identified by a green screw terminal) and the +5V rail is on the right.

Step #6: Wiring the Limit Switches

The limit switches are SPDT switches. They should be wired so that the “C” terminal is connected to ground and the “NC” terminal is connected to signal. By doing this, the limit switch will register high impedance when it is not triggered. This allows us to configure the limit switches so that the foram chassis reads high impedance whenever the limit switch is triggered or disconnected from the system (i.e. the stepper motors will not move when the limit switch is triggered or disconnected from the system).

To wire two limit switches on a single tic, connect the ground of both switches to the same ground pin on the tic. Connect the “NC” terminal to RX pin for one limit switch and TX pin for the other limit switch.

Step #7: Connect the motors and limit switches

  1. Air pumps: Connect to adafruit DC & Stepper Motor Bonnet
  1. Currently, there are three air pumps in the system.
  1. Stepper Motors: Connect to TIC T825 Stepper motor driver boards. Each T825 board can support one stepper motor.        
  1. Currently, there are two stepper motors (Actuonix S20-30-30-B) in the system.
  2. Limit Switches connect to TIC T825 motor boards. If you are using them as pull-up limit switches, then connect the limit switch ground to the GND pin on the T825 board and connect the limit switch signal to the “TX” pin on the T825 motor board. Each T825 motor board should have one limit switch attached to it.
  1. Servos: Connect to the Maestro Servo controller
  1. Currently, there are two servos in the system: HS-785HB and HS-485HB.
